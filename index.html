Designed from the inside out.

Gardener & Son is an ecological design studio based in Melbourne. We design gardens — and the living systems within them.

Each project begins with place: soil, slope, climate, culture, and the ecological community that once thrived there. We study context carefully, observing before we intervene. We work with contours rather than against them.

Gardens are not decorative backdrops.
They are fragments of a larger ecological whole.

They are also personal spaces — places of retreat, play, nourishment, and renewal. When people feel connected to their garden, care deepens. As care deepens, biodiversity follows.

Time spent in a living garden restores clarity, improves wellbeing, and fosters belonging. The garden becomes not something to maintain, but something to participate in.

How We Work

Every site is read before it is designed. Every material is chosen for honesty. Every garden is built to mature.

01
Begin on site

Design begins walking — reading slope, soil, light, and remnant pattern. Form emerges from context rather than being imposed upon it.

02
Favour continuity

Remnant vegetation, local geology, indigenous systems, and subtle cultural traces inform the work. We favour continuity over novelty.

03
Design to mature

Gardens are designed to grow over years. Season by season, they settle. Plants find rhythm. Soil deepens. Shade shifts. What begins as structure becomes relationship.

04
Measure and document

Measurement and documentation sit beside intuition and artistry. Craft, integrity, and systemic beauty guide how we work — and how we record what we've made.

The System

Infrastructure for private ecology.

Individual gardens matter — but systemic change requires infrastructure around them. Classification, measurement, registry, incentives, and aggregation are the layers that make private ecological work visible and durable.

About

Tyson & Natasha, Melbourne.

Gardener & Son was founded by Tyson and Natasha in Melbourne. The studio brings together ecological design, systems thinking, and the economic architecture required to scale restoration through private land.

Tyson works as ecological designer and systems thinker. Natasha as plant stylist and aesthetic visionary. Together the studio operates across garden design, measurement frameworks, registry infrastructure, and the cultural narrative required to shift how cities understand private ecology.

The studio is based at Churchill Street, Mont Albert — with a new studio space opening soon, including an indigenous plant nursery and laneway wine bar.

Building the private ecological infrastructure layer for cities.

This requires more than gardens. It requires registry infrastructure and economic frameworks that can recognise, value, and connect private ecological work — without losing the craft and beauty of the garden itself.

This is the ecological economy: stewardship with proof, and restoration that can compound across neighbourhoods. Long-horizon work. Built steadily.
